Objective

The objective of this project was to develop and execute test methods to qualify a new double-wide LEM shipping crate under realistic transport conditions. The goal was to simulate shipping loads and mishandling scenarios in order to evaluate whether the crate design was robust enough for deployment.

Constraints

Testing needed to replicate realistic shipping conditions while remaining safe, repeatable, and practical to execute in a manufacturing environment.

Key constraints included:

  • Simulating likely shipping and handling conditions without causing unnecessary damage to internal components.
  • Designing tests that were clearly documented and repeatable.
  • Following appropriate safety protocols during physical testing.
  • Capturing meaningful data that could support validation decisions.

Test Development

I developed structured test methods to evaluate the performance of the crate under simulated shipping conditions. This included defining test cases, identifying key variables, and building a clear test plan so that results could be interpreted consistently.

The process involved:

  • Designing test scenarios to simulate handling and transport loads.
  • Establishing clear procedures and documentation.
  • Coordinating test execution with others.
  • Planning ahead around equipment availability and scheduling constraints.

Execution and Analysis

Testing was conducted to simulate real-world shipping conditions and potential mishandling. I collected and interpreted data from each test in order to evaluate the structural performance of the crate and identify possible failure modes.

A key part of the project was not just running the tests, but forming clear conclusions from the results and using them to assess whether the crate design was suitable for shipping use.

Results & Impact

The testing provided a structured basis for evaluating the durability of the new crate design under realistic conditions.

Key outcomes:

  • Established a repeatable test methodology for crate qualification.
  • Improved understanding of how the crate responded to shipping-related loads.
  • Supported data-driven conclusions about the robustness of the design.
  • Reinforced the importance of clear planning, documentation, and safety in validation work.
