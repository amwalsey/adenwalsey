Electric Vehicle Body CFD Optimization

CFD-driven design study focused on reducing aerodynamic drag through vehicle body streamlining and rear-wake control.

Objective

Design and evaluate an electric vehicle body using CFD to reduce aerodynamic drag while maintaining practical geometric constraints. The project focused on understanding how body shape, rear taper, and wake behavior affect drag and vehicle efficiency.

Simulation Setup

The model used incompressible, steady-state airflow with a symmetry assumption across the vehicle width. Boundary conditions included a 27 m/s velocity inlet, pressure outlet, and moving ground plane. A k-ω GEKO turbulence model was used, with drag and lift coefficient monitors tracked over 200 iterations.

A tetrahedral mesh was generated with boundary-layer refinement near the car body to better capture near-wall flow behavior. A target Y+ value of approximately 50 was used to estimate the first cell height.

Design Exploration

The initial design produced a noticeable wake region behind the vehicle, contributing to pressure drag and motivating a smoother rear-end geometry. The refined design increased vehicle length from 5.0 m to 5.6 m and used a more gradual rear taper to keep flow attached longer and reduce wake size.

Results

The refined design reduced drag coefficient from 0.32 to 0.29, representing an approximate 9% improvement. Lift coefficient also decreased slightly from 0.27 to 0.26. While the reduction was smaller than expected, the results confirmed that rear-body streamlining can reduce flow separation and pressure drag.

  • Reduced drag coefficient from 0.32 to 0.29.
  • Reduced lift coefficient from 0.27 to 0.26.
  • Improved rear flow attachment through a longer, smoother taper.
  • Reduced but did not eliminate the low-pressure wake behind the vehicle.

Limitations and Next Steps

The study was limited by steady-state assumptions, turbulence model limitations, mesh resolution, and uncertainty in near-wall Y+ behavior. Future work would include a mesh independence study, improved Y+ monitoring, transient simulations, and more aggressive rear-end optimization.
